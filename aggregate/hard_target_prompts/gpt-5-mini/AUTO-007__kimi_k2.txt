<|im_assistant|>assistant<|im_middle|>## Section 2 — Plant Care Fundamentals 🌿

Building on the placement and occasion guidance from Section 1 (choose the right plant for the right moment), this section covers the everyday care that keeps those choices thriving. Think of these as repeatable habits: small, consistent actions that reduce stress for both plant and caregiver.

### Watering 💧
- Frequency vs. moisture: water based on substrate moisture, not calendar dates. Stick your finger 1–2" into the soil (or use a moisture meter); if it’s dry at that depth, it’s time.
- Techniques: top-watering for most houseplants, bottom-watering for thirstier specimens or when avoiding leaf splash. Allow excess water to drain; never let roots sit in standing water.
- Signs to watch for: yellow, limp leaves = overwatering; crispy brown edges = underwatering. If in doubt, err toward slightly drier than constantly soggy.

### Sunlight ☀️
- Know light levels: bright indirect, direct sun, and low light are distinct regimes. Match plant labels to the room’s actual light (observe an area for an hour at different times).
- Placement tips: east/west windows provide gentler morning/afternoon sun; south windows are strongest. Use sheers or rotate plants monthly to prevent lopsided growth.
- Acclimation: introduce plants to stronger light gradually over 2–3 weeks to avoid sunburn.

### Soil & Potting 🪴
- Mix matters: airy, well-draining mixes suit most houseplants. Add perlite or pumice for drainage; use higher organic content for moisture-loving species.
- pH & nutrients: most houseplants prefer slightly acidic to neutral pH (5.5–7). Fertilize lightly during active growth seasons using balanced, low-salt formulas.
- Repotting: small, incremental pot size increases every 12–24 months or when roots become pot-bound.

### Sustainable Practices ♻️
- Water-saving: capture rainwater, reuse drained water for non-sensitive plants, and group plants to reduce evaporation.
- Soil health: prioritize compost, worm castings, and local organic amendments over synthetic inputs.
- Waste reduction: propagate cuttings, donate extra plants, and choose biodegradable pots when possible to close the loop.

Victorian floriography: the language of flowers
- In the 19th century, floriography became a social code. Different blooms conveyed subtle messages when etiquette prevented direct speech: red roses for passionate love, yellow carnations for disdain, violets for modesty. These meanings were often regionally specific and shifted with fashion and botanical availability. As a responsible practitioner, note that Victorian assignments reflect their era’s gendered and class-bound norms; modern use should adapt meanings thoughtfully. 🌿

Cross-cultural meanings and caveats
- Symbolism is not universal. Chrysanthemums signify death in parts of Europe but represent longevity and joy in East Asia. The lotus is sacred in South Asia and East Asia, while the marigold plays central roles in Mexican Day of the Dead traditions. Always check cultural context before assigning or gifting symbolic arrangements; missteps can unintentionally disrespect traditions. 🌏

Modern symbolism and reclaimed meanings
- Contemporary floristry repurposes traditional symbolism: wildflowers can signal resilience and local biodiversity; native-plant bouquets convey ecological care; gendered associations are being softened as communities reclaim floral language for inclusion. Social movements also use flowers as symbols (e.g., poppies for remembrance, specific blooms in protest iconography). Sustainability-minded symbolism stresses provenance, pollinator value, and seasonality as part of the message.

Practical tips
